Context

In 2020, Bitcoin was the most talked-about investment in the world — and also the most feared. Its volatility made people hesitate. They waited for the perfect entry point, which meant they either never invested, or they panic-bought at peaks and panic-sold at dips. The result was the opposite of wealth building: buying high, selling low, and blaming Bitcoin for their losses.

Dollar cost averaging is the strategy that removes that decision entirely — invest a fixed amount at regular intervals, regardless of price, and let time do the work. But DCA was unknown to most new investors in 2020, and no tool existed to show them what it would look like in practice. Bitnob built one. I collaborated with another designer on the full experience — a web app that let users input an amount, a frequency, and a timeframe, and see exactly what their DCA journey would have looked like.

Tension

Designing a financial education tool for novice investors surfaces a specific set of tensions.

T1DCA is a simple concept — but explaining it to someone who barely understands Bitcoin requires making complexity feel approachable without oversimplifying to the point of being useless to intermediate or experienced investors.
T2The tool had to be educational and functional simultaneously — if it just explained DCA, it was a blog post. If it just calculated, it was a spreadsheet. The value lived in the combination.
T3Bitcoin's volatility is the very thing making users anxious — but it's also the data that makes the DCA argument most compelling. Showing historical price fluctuations had to build confidence, not reinforce fear.
T4Three distinct user groups — beginners, intermediate investors, and experienced traders — all had different literacy levels. One tool had to serve all three without alienating any of them.

"Education is crucial to designing a product for crypto. You can't design for a behaviour you don't understand, and you can't design for users who don't understand the behaviour either."

— Key learning from the research phase, reflected in the design approach

Decision

Research across three user groups and competitive analysis of four DCA tools shaped five design decisions:

  • Single, simplified input field for amount and frequency — reduced cognitive load to the minimum viable decision: how much, how often
    Tradeoff: less flexibility for power users, but the primary audience needed to act before they overthought it
  • Visual timeline showing the DCA journey over the user's chosen period — made the strategy tangible rather than theoretical
    Tradeoff: required close collaboration with developers on real-time data rendering, but a chart that responds to your inputs is infinitely more persuasive than a static example
  • Interactive graph showing historical DCA performance versus lump sum investing — the side-by-side comparison that made the core argument for DCA without requiring any explanation
    Tradeoff: the most technically complex element to build, but also the one that converted sceptics most effectively
  • Glossary of terms accessible throughout the experience — gave beginners a safety net without interrupting the flow for users who didn't need it
    Tradeoff: added surface area to the UI, but removed the anxiety of encountering unfamiliar financial terminology mid-calculation
  • Positioned the tool as a Bitnob-adjacent product — educational enough to stand alone, but with a clear path to downloading the Bitnob app for users ready to act
    Tradeoff: required the tool to earn trust independently before making the ask, but a calculator that tries to sell too early loses the user it was trying to convert
Final product — calculator with graph
Outcome

The Bitnob DCA calculator attracted 50,000+ unique visitors in its first quarter, achieved a 45% conversion rate to Bitnob app download, and 60% of converted users went on to start their DCA journey through Bitnob. For a free educational tool with no paid acquisition, those numbers validated the core thesis: teach users something valuable and they'll trust you with their investment.

Reflection

This project taught me that financial education is a design problem, not a content problem. The information about DCA existed before this tool — it was in articles, YouTube videos, and Reddit threads. What didn't exist was a way to make that information personally relevant. The moment a user typed in their own amount and frequency and saw their own projected journey, DCA stopped being a concept and became a plan.

Starting with zero personal understanding of DCA and having to research it before designing for it also shaped how I approached the brief. Understanding the user's confusion requires first experiencing it yourself. That's a principle I've carried into every financial product I've worked on since.
