Context

Raenest serves freelancers across Africa and emerging economies who earn in foreign currencies, particularly those on platforms like Upwork. The existing withdrawal experience was functional, but slow — it took 24–48 hours to process funds after a withdrawal request. For a freelancer managing rent, a supplier payment, or unexpected expenses, that gap is a real financial problem.

The business opportunity was equally clear. If Raenest could offer near-instant payouts while competitors couldn't, it would become the default account for any serious Upwork freelancer. FastTrack was that bet — a new standalone feature powered by Rollee's API integration that would make funds available in under an hour.

I came in as lead product designer, partnering closely with a PM who owned the product brief, and working within the technical boundaries set by how Rollee's integration with Upwork actually functioned. I worked closely with the engineering team to understand the boundaries of what Rollee's integration could support — their constraints became design inputs, not blockers.

Tension

The core design challenge wasn't the speed — engineering handled that. It was this: to use FastTrack, users had to authenticate with Upwork inside a Raenest feature. That handoff, however briefly, breaks the illusion of a single-trusted product. And when you're asking someone to connect their income source to a third-party service, that moment of uncertainty is where people abandon.

At the same time, the Rollee integration had a fixed technical structure. The flow couldn't be redesigned around user preferences — it had to work within what the integration allowed. So the tension wasn't "what's the ideal flow?" It was: how do you make a technically constrained integration feel safe, simple, and native to Raenest?

T1 Trust at the handoff — users authenticating with Upwork inside Raenest breaks the single-product illusion at exactly the moment they're most anxious about their money.
T2 Technical constraints as design input — Rollee's integration had a fixed authentication sequence that couldn't be reordered, regardless of user preference.
T3 No prior mental model — users had never connected a third-party platform to Raenest before. The design had to explain a new behaviour without losing momentum.

"The integration gives us the rails. Your job is to make sure nobody notices they're switching tracks."

— PM conversation that framed the design brief
Exploration / Wireframes
Decision

Rather than treating the constraints as limitations, I used them as a design brief. The Rollee documentation became a design input — understanding exactly where the handoff happened let me design deliberately around that moment instead of around it. Four decisions shaped the final flow:

  • Designed the flow as a standalone feature, not an add-on to the existing withdrawal screen
    Tradeoff: more surface area to design, but gave full control over the trust experience end-to-end
  • Used Raenest's design language throughout — same components, same typography, same color — so the Upwork handoff felt like a continuation, not a departure
    Tradeoff: constrained visual creativity, but maintained the single-product illusion users needed to feel safe
  • Added explicit dual-logo trust signals and plain-language explanation at the authentication step — showing both Raenest and Upwork logos together, with a one-sentence explanation of what was happening and why
    Tradeoff: adds a screen, but research showed this was the highest drop-off risk moment
  • Designed every failure and edge case before any happy path screen was finalised — failed connections, pending states, partial transfers, timeout errors
    Tradeoff: doubled the design scope, but a feature about moving money cannot ship with ambiguous failure states

Outcome

Within 4 months of launch, FastTrack processed $2.4M for 10,985 customers — with no dedicated marketing push at launch. The adoption curve was organic, driven by word of mouth among the Upwork freelancer community. Withdrawal time dropped from up to 48 hours to consistently under 1 hour.

The volume of money moved in that window — for a brand new feature — confirmed that the pent-up demand was real and that the trust design worked. Users didn't just try it once. The repeat usage rate in the first two months indicated the flow had earned confidence, not just curiosity.

Reflection

This project sharpened something I now apply to every financial product I design: trust is not a feature you add — it's a property of every decision in the flow. The moment I stopped thinking about the Rollee integration as a technical constraint and started thinking of it as a trust design problem, the whole project became clearer.

If I were to revisit this, I'd invest more time in post-launch qualitative research — specifically, interviewing users who completed FastTrack for the first time. The metrics show it worked, but I'd want to understand which specific moment made them feel safe enough to proceed. That insight would make the next version significantly stronger.
