Context

In 2021, cryptocurrency adoption across Africa was growing fast, but holding Bitcoin has historically meant choosing between two options: keep it and watch it grow, or sell it for liquidity when you need cash. Bitnob saw a different possibility: what if the Bitcoin sitting in a user's wallet could unlock real liquidity without them having to sell it? NobCredit was that bet — Africa's first crypto-backed lending feature, built directly into the Bitnob app.

The product's promise was genuinely novel: borrow dollars against your Bitcoin, repay on your own terms, and watch your collateral continue to grow in value while it's locked. No long forms, no branch visits, no credit history checks — just your crypto and a few taps. As the sole designer, my remit was to design an experience compelling enough to convert Bitnob's existing crypto users into first-time borrowers — for a product category that had never existed in the African market. Throughout the project I worked closely with the engineering and risk teams, as their requirements on data collection shaped which fields made the cut and which were cut entirely.

Product screens — overview
Tension

Designing Africa's first crypto-backed loan meant there was no existing user mental model to lean on. Every tension was amplified by novelty.

T1The concept of crypto as collateral was completely new to most users — the design had to educate and convert simultaneously, without feeling like a tutorial.
T2The business needed enough user data for credit assessment — but NobCredit's core promise was "no long forms." Every field was a potential contradiction of the product's own value proposition.
T3Flexible repayment — borrow as much as you like, repay at any time, vary your amounts — is a powerful differentiator, but genuinely complex to communicate without triggering confusion or anxiety about debt.
T4NobCredit was an unknown brand asking users to trust it with their financial identity data. Trust had to be earned by the design itself — there was no brand equity to lean on.

"Users already trust Bitnob with their Bitcoin. Now we're asking them to trust us with their financial decisions. That's a different kind of trust."

— Insight from product stakeholder session
Wireframes / Exploration
Decision

The design had to earn trust progressively — not demand it upfront. Two principles resolved most of the tension. Everything else followed from them.

  • Progressive disclosure throughout — surfaced only what the user needed at each step, keeping the "no long forms" promise intact while collecting what the business required
    Tradeoff: required careful sequencing across more screens, but preserved the core product promise at every step
  • Designed the collateral mechanic as a feature, not a footnote — the fact that your Bitcoin keeps growing while locked was positioned as a key value driver on the loan summary screen
    Tradeoff: added complexity to the summary screen, but turned a technical detail into a genuine selling point
  • Plain-language explainers at every financial decision point — interest, repayment flexibility, collateral release — written for a first-time borrower, not a crypto-native
    Tradeoff: added copy length to key screens, but research on comparable products showed comprehension directly correlates with completion
  • Used familiar visual patterns from established Nigerian fintech products to reduce the "unknown brand" friction
    Tradeoff: less visual differentiation, but a first-time borrower needs pattern recognition more than novelty. Trust before delight.
Final screens — loan flow
Outcome

NobCredit disbursed over $2 million in dollar loans and contributed 2.5% of Bitnob's total revenue — making a first-of-its-kind product in Africa into a measurable business asset. The flexible repayment model saw strong repeat usage, validating the decision to foreground borrower autonomy over rigid repayment structures.

The mixed user base — salaried employees and small business owners — both completed applications through the same core flow, which validated the decision to use progressive disclosure over separate user journeys. Designing one flow that served two mental models was a deliberate bet that paid off.

Reflection

This project taught me that in lending, the form is the product. Unlike a transfer or a payment — where the UI is a wrapper around a fast action — a loan application is a sustained commitment of attention and trust. Every screen is an opportunity to either reinforce that trust or break it.

If I were rebuilding this today, I'd instrument the drop-off points more rigorously during the design phase — defining in advance which steps I expected to lose users and by how much. That would have given post-launch data sharper meaning and made the second iteration faster to prioritise.
