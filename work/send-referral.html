Send App Referral: a reward both sides forgot to claim

Improving the referral experience on Send App to increase conversions by fixing discoverability, reward clarity, and sharing friction.

Context

Send App had a referral programme with a genuinely compelling mechanic: refer someone and you both get $20. In a remittance product used daily by people with active networks of family and friends who needed the same service, that's a strong growth proposition. The business had already committed to the reward. The infrastructure existed. The only thing missing was a design that made any of it visible.

Low referral usage data triggered a stakeholder brief, and user interviews confirmed what the analytics suggested: most users didn't know the programme existed. Those who found it couldn't clearly understand what they or their referee would receive. And even motivated users encountered enough friction in the sharing flow to abandon before completing a referral. As sole designer, I was brought in to diagnose and fix all three. I worked across the product, UX writing, and engineering teams throughout — the reward language in particular came out of close collaboration with the UX writer to make the $20/$20 incentive feel concrete, not vague.

Tension

The existing page carried too much, and the wrong things were at the top.

T1The page led with a rewards balance of $0 USD and a greyed-out Redeem button. The first thing a new user saw was a reminder that they hadn't done anything yet. It was the least motivating possible opening.
T2The share link appeared before the reward explanation — users were being asked to act before understanding why. Comprehension had to precede the call to action, not follow it.
T3The page included "My referrals", "Reward withdrawals", terms and conditions banners, and FAQs — content that mattered after a user had already referred someone, not before. First-time users were navigating a page designed for repeat users.
T4The Share Link button sat outside the natural thumb reach zone — ergonomically awkward for one-handed use, which is how most users interact with a remittance app in motion.

"The first page is meant to just have the major things important for referral: invitation link, terms and conditions, what the referrer and referee get, and FAQs."

— From the original design brief annotations
Decision

The redesign rebuilt the page hierarchy around one question: what does a first-time user need to see, in what order, to go from landing on this page to sharing a link?

  • Led with the reward — "YOU GET $20 / THEY GET $20" as the page headline, visible before any scroll, any reading, any decision
    Tradeoff: displaced the rewards balance and redeem button from the top position, but a $0 balance with a greyed button is demotivating — the incentive to act belongs first
  • Restructured the page to four elements only for first-time users: invitation link, what you get, what they get, terms and conditions — everything else removed from the primary view
    Tradeoff: less information density, but every element left on the page had a direct role in getting a first-time user to share
  • Applied thumb zone analysis to reposition the Share Link button to the natural reach zone — moving the primary CTA from the upper screen to the lower natural zone where one-handed interaction is comfortable
    Tradeoff: pushed secondary content further down, but the action that generates referrals had to be the easiest action on the page
  • Changed the homepage entry point label from "Referrals" to "Get $20" — so the incentive was communicated at the moment of discovery, not after the user had already decided to investigate
    Tradeoff: less descriptive, but value-first labelling converts; a label that describes a category does not

Outcome

The redesign launched in 2024. Post-launch conversion data was not directly accessible, but the diagnostic framework — four distinct failure modes addressed through four specific design decisions — gives this case study its value. A well-funded growth feature was failing entirely because of design, not product. The fix required no new mechanics, no new engineering, and no new reward budget. Just honest diagnosis and precise execution.

The most useful thing this project taught me: information architecture is a conversion tool, not an organisational one. The old page wasn't wrong about what to include — it was wrong about who it was organising that information for. Redesigning it for a first-time user, rather than a returning one, required removing more than adding.

Reflection

This project reinforced something I now apply to every redesign brief: before touching any screen, map every failure mode. The referral programme wasn't broken — it had three separate design problems that each needed their own solution. Treating them as one problem would have produced one solution that fixed none of them completely.

If I were revisiting this, I'd instrument each fix independently — measuring the entry point change separately from the reward communication change separately from the button repositioning. That granularity would tell you which lever had the biggest impact and where to focus version two.
